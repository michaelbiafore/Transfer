Welcome and How Grantham Got Started

[~0:00]

Cold open (Jeremy Grantham): “Great market peaks like this are traditionally followed by the worst of times — so don’t kid yourself. The great exception is 2022. In 2022, all the conditions of a major bubble are met, and it breaks: the S&P drops 25%, the Mag 7 drops 40, growth stocks about 30–35, and the bond market has the worst year in its history. But then ChatGPT comes out. Whatever you say about it, it’s pretty obvious it’s a game-changer, a life-changer — an important idea. It may make us all inevitably rich; it may destroy us all, take our jobs, or even inadvertently stomp on us. And it’s the first bubble that was interrupted by anything that powerful.”

Host: Jeremy, I’m really grateful to you for taking the time — you’re on holiday in Scotland, and we’re recording this in Edinburgh. You’ve got this wonderful book that tells the story of how you got started in investing. Half my guests want to buy their first stock with their newspaper-round money, and half go into it by accident — but you’re somewhere in between.

Jeremy Grantham: I got into it for the best possible reason, which was that I was looking to have a good time. I’d just graduated from business school and bought some time going into management consulting — and you don’t have to be very smart to realize that’s a bit of a waste of time. So I spent a year asking my classmates which business was the most fun, and by a huge amount, that was investing. It was a little small-cap shooting-star market, and that’s a lot of fun. My friends were beginning analysts having so much fun, I couldn’t wait to join them.

I ran a fairly professional campaign — a list of 50 people to apply to. I had a couple of very interesting offers: a fancy hedge-fund dude in pinstriped suits and pocket handkerchiefs, everyone’s cool hero; another in London, very enterprising. But both were held up by boring real-world things, like the registration of a new fund. Finally I took a fairly prosaic offer from a mutual-fund group in Boston called Keystone — which at the time was $1.6 billion versus Fidelity’s $1.7 billion, if you can believe that. Keystone fizzled out and Fidelity inherited the world. When I interviewed at Fidelity, the guy was so hyper that he had one of those early Bunker Ramo machines, and as he talked to me with one eye, he checked his stocks with the other. I didn’t have the nerve to walk out. So I went to Keystone — and sure enough, investing was a lot of fun.

The 1973-74 Crash

[~3:39]

Host: I like to interview older investors because you’ve seen more cycles. The only other person I know who lived through the ’73–’74 crash as an investor was Mario Gabelli.

Jeremy Grantham: Oh, that was brutal. We used to semi-joke that the main problem in ’73–’74 was getting your weary ass to work — left foot forward, right foot forward. Everyone was slightly depressed. The little guys went down, the big guys went down, quality went down, junk went down — everything went down, rotating week by week, lower and lower. “Oh my god, the big chemical companies yield 6%, surely that will put the brake on” — and it appeared to, for a week or two. Then 7%, 8%, 9%, 10% — finally things like Celanese and Monsanto were yielding 10%, and at a lowly seven-and-a-half P/E it stopped going down. But everyone was pulverized, and there was rapid inflation.

“In real terms, the market went down about 65% and took no prisoners. By everyone’s agreement, it was the worst one, second only to the Great Depression.”

The Bond Market and the Folly of Extrapolation

[~5:00]

Host: There are a number of parallels with today, aren’t there?

Jeremy Grantham: There was an oil crisis — and every serious spike in oil has been followed by a recession and a stock-market decline. That one was the biggest of all spikes: a two-pronged effort by the Middle Eastern powers to deliberately drive the price up, with lines of cars at the gas stations. Out of that came rapid inflation and very high rates. The yield on the 30-year bond peaked around ’82 at 16%. You can understand a T-bill peaking at 13% if inflation is over 10%, and a three-point premium of the long bond over T-bills. But what you can’t understand is that, for 30 years, they’d price in the highest level of inflation that had ever occurred.

That’s quite a reveal: the bond market is not predicting the future. A group of economists had decided long-term inflation was 4.5% — so a 16% long bond appeared to represent 11–12% real for 30 years. It was obvious the bond market was just extrapolating the condition of the day as if it would stay that way forever. As Keynes would say, even though we know from personal experience that’s not the case, extrapolation is something we adopt to deal with difficult times. And his point was the cardinal rule:

“Never be wrong on your own. If you extrapolate today’s conditions and everybody does it, you’re all on the same page — and that’s the thing that really matters in career risk. All have the same thoughts, all do the same thing, and no one loses their job.”

Peak Margins Times Peak PE

[~8:12]

Host: Do you think we’re doing the same extrapolation today?

Jeremy Grantham: Absolutely — that hasn’t changed. Every bull market is peak profit margins: 1929, 2000, 2022, today, and almost in the housing bubble of ’07. Profit margins always used to be mean-reverting — high returns attract capital and drive them down, and vice versa. So you’d think that if margins are abnormally high, you’d be very careful about the P/E you apply. That is not how the real world works.

“1929: peak profits times peak P/E of 21. 1972, the Nifty Fifty: 21 times earnings again, times peak margins. 2000: world-record profit margins times 35 times earnings. They celebrated the new high in margins by multiplying by a new high in P/E — double-counting of the worst variety.”

By contrast, in 1974 you had crushed oil, crushed margins, multiplied by seven-and-a-half times earnings. In ’82, eight times earnings on crushed margins. And in 2009, after the crash, a dramatic drop in earnings times the lowest P/E we’d had in 22 years.

Shiller, Volatility, and Career Risk

[~9:55]

Jeremy Grantham: This is why Shiller found that if you were clairvoyant about actual future earnings and dividends and discounted them back, the real stock market was about 17 times more volatile than was justified by the fairly steady path of actual earnings and dividends we were supposedly discounting. We were not doing that at all — we were constantly extrapolating today’s conditions, much too high, much too low, riding the cycle.

You’d think, once you’ve established that, how easy it must be to beat the market. But it isn’t — because the career risk of playing the logical game (short the bubbles, long the busts) is nerve-wracking. The uncertainty in the cycle, both up and down, is longer than the client’s patience. In a bull market the client’s patience contracts, and you get fired before you’re right.

“You will never hear a major sensible firm — Goldman Sachs, JP Morgan — tell you to get your tail out of the market. They never have, they never will. It’s simply a terrible commercial strategy.”

Buy-and-Hold and the Half-the-Time Recovery

[~11:44]

Host: It’s almost worse today, with commentators telling you you’re guaranteed 8% a year in the S&P 500 irrespective of valuation.

Jeremy Grantham: You always had that. In 1929 a famous Ladies’ Home Journal article said all you needed to invest was $50,000 and you were rich for life. Jeremy Siegel, now dean of Wharton, wrote an almost identical article in the 2000 tech bubble — just ignore the cycles, tuck the stocks away, and get rich. And if you have a 50-year horizon, that will do just fine, because the market compounds a little higher than makes sense — about 8% real is a very handsome return.

On the other hand, half of all the time is spent climbing back to the old high. 1929 doesn’t get back there until about 1955. The Nifty Fifty crash of ’72 doesn’t recover until ’85–’86. It’s almost exactly half the time. People think you’re always going up — you’re not. You’re going up to new highs half the time, and waiting to recover from a decent setback the other half.

The Highest Market in History

[~13:23]

Host: How long will it take to recover from the next bust? We’re at quite high levels.

Jeremy Grantham: I always tout Hussman — I’ve never met him, but his data is very much like ours used to be. His most reliable forecaster is a cousin of Warren Buffett’s indicator: the total price of the market compared to GDP. On that basis, Hussman would say this is the highest market in history, above 2000, which is the contender.

“The terrible thing about peak markets is they don’t predict much better than average times — they are always followed by the worst of times. 1929, priced higher than ever, was followed by the Depression. 1972 was followed by a nasty recession, oil crisis, and collapsing stocks. Great market peaks like this are traditionally followed by the worst of times — so don’t kid yourself.”

How ChatGPT Interrupted the 2022 Bear Market

[~15:06]

Jeremy Grantham: The great exception is 2022. All the conditions of a major bubble were met, and it broke: the S&P dropped 25%, the Mag 7 dropped 40, growth stocks about 30–35, and the bond market had the worst year in its history. An ugly year. But then in November, ChatGPT came out — and whatever you say about it, it’s obviously a game-changer, a life-changer, an important idea. It may make us all inevitably rich; it may destroy us all, take our jobs, or inadvertently stomp on us. It’s the first bubble interrupted by anything that powerful. It would take a war, a giant virus, or AI to do that.

We had a nice recession going, the economy weakening — and it was interrupted by massive investment in AI, second only to the railroads as a fraction of GDP, an increment of about 2% on GDP. Had the drift down in animal spirits (à la Keynes) kept going, we could easily have fallen into at least a mild recession, the market could have gone down another 20%, and I’d have been just about happy. But the Mag 7 didn’t just go up — they doubled. The rest of the market drifted, then almost a year later threw in the towel and decided the Mag 7 knew what they were doing. Back to a new high we came.

The Worst Global Environment Ever

[~16:41]

Jeremy Grantham: The thing about this one is that the global environment is the worst it’s ever been. With clairvoyance you knew other things had been worse — if World War II arrived, it would be worse. But in real life you didn’t know World War I or II was coming; it took odd behavior — archdukes being shot, Hitler invading Poland. This time we have Ukraine, the Middle East, Taiwan lurking over everybody’s head, trade wars, tariff craziness beyond belief. International trade has carried the world since World War II — everyone benefits — and now we’re bound and determined to put it into reverse.

So you have geopolitics, global trade, and behind that, climate change. I’ve worried about it for 25 years, but only in the last two has it become big enough to impact global GDP — by about half a percent a year. The number of billion-dollar-plus disasters — storms, floods, droughts, fires — has gone through the roof. And critically, insurance is becoming higher-priced and in some cases impossible to get. Insurance is very central to capitalism.

Why No One Calls Turning Points

[~19:42]

Host: Why has the market shrugged this off? The VIX has collapsed; people seem relaxed.

Jeremy Grantham: We don’t do that kind of stuff very well. In the late summer of ’29, business was turning down rapidly, but the data lagged a couple of months, so people were relaxed and about as optimistic as ever — and then wham. We’re not good at predicting turning points. I have a theory: it’s not a bug, it’s built into the system. The great enterprises — corporate and nonprofit, with hundreds of thousands of employees — are, by definition, led by people with very high political skills. Keynes was clear: the main thing a politician must know is never be wrong on your own.

“At any major turning point, the engine room may be screaming that there’s a major vulnerability — but the spokespeople will never see it. They’ll always wait and see what other people are doing, and then jump. So no one ever calls turning points.”

And therefore an individual, who has much less career risk, is free to look at the data, see the giant bubbles appearing, and act. You have to be brain-dead not to see it. An institution cannot do that and will not advise you to.

Greenspan, Feet of Clay, and the Fed Put

[~21:56]

Host: You make the point that bubbles have a net adverse economic impact — usually followed by a capex bust. Why don’t regulators try to temper the enthusiasm? Everyone talks about Greenspan in reverent tones —

Jeremy Grantham: Not me. Greenspan — I wrote a paper called Feet of Clay; I thought he was laughably wrong. My first encounter with him: he ran an investment-advisory firm so bad that, when I’m feeling mean, I say he was the only person ever laughed out of business. He was used as a joke. Then he disappeared, bootlicking down in Washington, and reappeared as chairman of the Fed. He got everything wrong, and he invented the asymmetric Fed behavior: if anything goes wrong, we’ll bail you out; if anything goes right, you’re on your own.

“We’ll socialize the costs, privatize the gains.”

Bernanke and Yellen were acolytes; the present guy, not so bad. They tried every trick to create chain-link bubbles by letting everybody know the Fed had your back — why wouldn’t that create bubbles? It did.

Why Great Ideas Guarantee Bubbles

[~24:00]

Jeremy Grantham: The more obviously wonderful an idea is, the easier it is to suck in too much money. Railroads lost everyone’s money even though they changed the world. The internet lost everybody money — Amazon, the great hero, had gone up six or seven times in 18 months and then lost 92% in the decline, as I like to say (check it) — and then inherited the world. It’s not that the ideas don’t work out in the long run; it’s that great ideas like AI are guaranteed to suck in more money, because everyone can see it will change the world.

“‘My $100 billion capex is bigger than your $87 billion.’ What does that guarantee? A bubble in AI capex — like fiber-optic cable. And no one ever predicts it.”

Host: If you can see it with your own eyes, why wouldn’t you act on it?

Jeremy Grantham: The trouble is the timing. But that’s not a problem for an individual — it’s your money; you get out a little early, who cares? It’s a huge risk for an institution. The market’s uncertainty is occasionally longer than the client’s patience, so you’ll sooner or later be wrong — and as Keynes said, “you will not receive much mercy.” We can vouch for that: in 2000, doing the right thing for the right reasons and winning the bet, we nevertheless lost two-thirds of our market share in two and a quarter years. We went from $30 billion to $20 billion while everyone else went from 30 to 45 or 60.

Feathers in a Hurricane and the 50% Fall

[~27:45]

Jeremy Grantham: The market is outrageously hard to predict unless you take a long-term view. You know it will come down; you don’t know when. I used to get grief for that — “the long term is just a series of short terms; if you can’t do period A or B, you can’t do the collection.” Then I found the example in the book: you stand atop a high-rise in Florida in a hurricane and throw feathers from a big bag. Some land a block away in half a minute; some, like poor canaries from Jamaica, get swirled away and end up in Maine days later.

“You know nothing about the short term, but you know something about the long term: sooner or later, you can guarantee every feather will hit the ground. That’s like a bubble — a gravitational pull. You know with certainty that sooner or later it will come down.”

Host: You wrote that all 27 bubbles you examined returned to the previous trend. Where do you expect the S&P to be when it cracks?

Jeremy Grantham: The trend is, I mean — it’s pretty painful — at least down by half.

Host: A 50% fall.

Jeremy Grantham: All the great bubbles are 50% plus. They then take quite a bit of time to get back — variable. The Depression was 25 years; the shortest was six or seven.

The Narrowness Signal

[~30:36]

[Aside] Sponsor reads for Behind the Balance Sheet (an investment-training consultancy and online school) and AlphaSense (document search/Q&A for investors).

Host: Your bubble detector looks at the narrowness of the market — the thinning out before the top. Why?

Jeremy Grantham: This is intelligent guesswork. The last great signal before the bust in ’29: very aggressive, flaky little stocks without much earnings, all up 80% in 1928 — then in early January 1929 they start going down as the S&P powers upwards. The day before the crash, the S&P’s low-priced index was down almost 40%.

“That’s the primal scream from the stomach of the stock market of all time.”

High-beta stocks do not go down when the market goes up — except in 1929. Nothing like it again until 1972, the top of the Nifty Fifty: the S&P went up 17% and the average stock went down 17%. Then 2000: in March, growth stocks broke and hurtled down 50% (led by Cisco), while the S&P hit a co-equal high in September — meaning everything that wasn’t a growth stock was up 13–14%. It rang the signal: watch your tail.

Nothing like that again until 2022 — when I owned a huge, unusual position in QuantumScape, a venture-capital company doing solid-state batteries that came public as a SPAC (which I think should be illegal). It came at $10 (four times my money), and in three months it was $131 — bigger than General Motors, still several years from any sales. It was a meme stock, but I was so close I couldn’t think; I wasn’t allowed to sell for six months. By the time the lockup ran out it was $25, and later went as low as $4. Then Cathie Wood’s portfolio of junior growth stocks dropped, down 30–35% by the end of ’22 with the S&P strongly up. Another signal, bang — only the fourth in history — and the market dutifully collapsed in ’22, before being rudely interrupted by AI.

Surviving the Value Drought

[~35:40]

Host: Value has been terribly painful — five, 10, 15 years. What’s your advice to people wedded to that philosophy when it’s not working?

Jeremy Grantham: The trivial but serious answer is: you go out of business. Value, done correctly, is always a sensible way to manage money — not just buying the cheapest assets. Price-to-book is the market’s way of saying “these are the assets I think are worst”; a good dividend-discount or multifactor model should always be used in a sensible world. But sensible investing hit a spectacularly long run — owing to Greenspan and the boys setting the fuse, then everyone going along, then the COVID bailout providing money beyond everyone’s wildest dreams. People sat at home with big checks and nothing to do but speculate — comparing notes online, “to the moon, to the moon.” We had speculation like never before, year after year.

GMO has survived, though it hasn’t thrived being as sensible as it could. We had a very nice year last year staying away from the US — emerging and developed ex-US had a splendid year, and a good year this year too. These cycles tend to be multi-year, so maybe we’ve seen the turn: the US probably faces a 10-year decline (with rallies). Just to remind you: in 2000, GMO’s 10-year forecast had emerging at the top (+11–12% a year) and the S&P at the very bottom (−1.5 to −2%). Ten years later, emerging beat its forecast (close to +13%), the S&P came in at −3%, and the gap was over 200 percentage points in favor of emerging. Those things happen, and they last.

Career Risk and the Clients Who Never Came Back

[~43:52]

Host: You lost half your clients in the dot-com boom, then prospered in the bust — but you wrote, “Of the clients that fired us, not one came back.”

Jeremy Grantham: Not one. The problem for an allocator is: how do you tell the difference between a manager who’s early and one who’s wrong? Later, time reveals it. People say “early is wrong” — fine, but then what’s the alternative? Perfect timing, or do nothing ever. No one has perfect timing, so everyone stays together and runs off the cliff together.

Host: If you were an allocator, what would you look for in a manager — intelligence or character?

Jeremy Grantham: I’d look for logic and great data. Do they sound sensible? Have they analyzed the data correctly? Have they got some insight into how the market works?

We genuinely looked after our clients — everyone says that, but dear listener, you know it’s not true: they look after their profits. If we thought a client wanted to do something bad for them — buy into Japan at the peak — we’d say no. By the way, the engine rooms of the great firms all agreed with me: at an epic financial-analyst meeting of 1,200 people, I got 400 equity experts to vote, and they almost unanimously believed the market (then at a P/E of 32) would go back to 17.5 within 10 years, and that this would guarantee a major bear market. Less than 1% disagreed. The people who employed them were on the podium with me saying, “Oh Jeremy, don’t be hysterical.” That’s how the industry works — the engine room knows the score.

Building GMO: Recruiting and Grantham’s Law

[~45:52]

Host: You recruited amazing people — James Montier, Edward Chancellor, who helped with the book. How?

Jeremy Grantham: Largely luck. I was mesmerized by the game, happy to delegate — a lazy bum who let other people do the hiring. I think I only ever offered a job to six people, including Edward Chancellor and James Montier. Ben Inker I didn’t offer — I was told he had to join by David Swensen of Yale, our most famous client, so being a man of great principle, I said, “Yes, sir,” and hired him. Andrew Smithers turned me down — I was so impressed by his ruthless logic that I asked him to come brainstorm; he said no, happy as a clam, and laughed about it around town ever after. (He’s responsible for the Credit Suisse — now UBS — Global Investment Returns Yearbook, an amazing publication.) Chris Darnell was a genius who finished top of his Yale year and was in danger of flunking out of Harvard Business School — perfect, because a business school is a trade school with low intellectual content, and for a mathematician it must have been boring. He joined us despite a fabulous offer elsewhere — my one real recruiting success.

What attracted them is that, obviously, we were not in the profit-maximizing business. We didn’t have a single person whose job was sales for 22 years. We did what was interesting and challenging, then checked whether clients agreed. That gives rise to Grantham’s law number two:

“If you’re doing well, you can sell any new product. If you’re doing badly, you can’t sell any new product, however promising.”

My job description was bouncing in and out of everyone’s room asking, “What is your idea this week?” — until I came to Edward, who’d say, “Get lost, I have a deadline.”

[On his own career arc, in roughly 15-year blocks with five-year overlaps:] Stock-picking in small-cap value was heaven — no insider information, we were the professionals, CEOs were pathetically grateful for a call. After 15 years, if I’d listened to one more broker about quarterly sales, I’d have thrown up. Then quant investing — a totally different kind of thinking (momentum versus value, regression rates) — terrific for five or six years. Then asset allocation — different again. And the final one was propaganda: quarterly letters and papers on running out of resources, climate change, and toxicity.

The Foundation: Resource Shortages

[~58:12]

Host: Tell us about the three areas of your foundation and why you chose them.

Jeremy Grantham: Resource shortages started as a purely intellectual idea — we noticed a pattern. Everyone’s view of resources was dominated by oil, which isn’t useful, so we took the 35 most important commodities, equal-weighted, and asked what resources had really done. Even with worse-and-worse copper ore, technology kept winning: the equation was roughly shortage/quality minus two a year, technology plus three a year. So for 100 years the price fell 1% a year — down 70% real over a century. What a help to getting rich.

Then, from 2002 to 2011, we had “World War II without World War II” — prices surged back to where they’d come from, which was China. China went in 25 years from 5% of cement to 50%, from 7% of iron ore to 50% — consuming half of every bag of cement, every ton of coal. That broke the back of commodities as everyone brought on their last best reserves. Our paper was called Time to Wake Up: Days of Abundant Resources and Falling Prices Are Over Forever. We’re now in a world where shortage slightly wins instead of technology — prices are about three times the 2002 low, in a volatile sideways phase, and we’re running out.

As Kenneth Boulding said, “anyone who believes you can have compound growth in a finite world is either a madman or an economist.” We are hurtling through space — spaceship earth — gobbling through a finite inheritance, particularly natural gas and oil.

The Foundation: Climate Change and Clean Tech

[~1:03:01]

Jeremy Grantham: The next was climate change. My whole family got involved as we traveled and saw how rapidly the world was disintegrating. The carbon count, which used to be 280, is now 430, heading to maybe 550 with our half-baked efforts — and at 550 we’d be lucky to hold 3°C up from today’s 1.5°C. The sudden increase in damage — floods, droughts, storms — has knocked perhaps half a percent off global GDP in the last couple of years and now impacts insurance, which is hardwired into capitalism. Someone worked out that, in the last 20 years, about a third of our GDP growth was correcting or avoiding natural disasters — and that number gets bigger until it’s 130%.

“But that’s not the end of the world. We’re a very rich world; we can take a few decades going back a bit, if we use that time to keep improving.”

And we have improved: wind, solar, and battery storage are much better than we ever expected. In 80% of the world, wind/solar/storage is simply cheaper than coal or natural gas — cheaper even if you gave them the coal plant for free, because just delivering the coal costs more than building a solar farm from scratch. EVs are very encouraging: over 400 miles of range, on the cusp of 500, charging to 80% in 10–12 minutes. The greening of the economy is trillions of dollars of opportunity, and we have a big chunk of our portfolio in green tech, driven by America’s venture-capital industry — the crown jewel of its rather wobbly capitalist system.

[Aside] Grantham jokes that he once hired a young man who was going to be number seven at Microsoft and had been offered shares — convincing him that “another flaky tech firm” was riskier than the investment business. “Cost him hundreds of millions a year.”

Toxicity, Fertility, and Demographics

[~1:08:40]

Jeremy Grantham: We don’t do well listening to unpleasant news. That applied to climate change — 25 years ago there was nothing but eye-rolling — but that’s nothing compared to toxicity. Toxicity is more dangerous, moving faster, and totally avoided. We’ve created a toxic stew; insect life is going out of business, and all the great experts, like E.O. Wilson (the famous ant scientist — I spent four hours bullying him at 90, probably killed him off), believe that without insects nature cascades, and without nature we don’t have a world suitable for humans.

We’ve designed a world with our noses rubbed against these toxins. Plastics leach endocrine disruptors that mess with our hormones and fertility. Sperm count is one of the few things you can measure accurately: in hunter-gatherer days, ~200 units per milliliter; by 1972, ~100; now 35 — dropping almost 2.5% a year in the 21st century, accelerating. Worse, many endocrine disruptors have an epigenetic effect — not just you, but your children and grandchildren, with recent studies suggesting up to 20 generations.

The declining count didn’t matter while we were “over-engineered like a good Victorian bridge” — until it hit a threshold ~17 years ago. The World Health people now say about 17–18% of young couples need help; the dominant expert, Shanna Swan, says that in 20–25 years the median young couple will need help — and beyond that, the viability of the species is at risk.

It also affects sex drive — exactly what endocrine disruption does to rats. One Japanese study of 8,000 people aged 20–50 asked how many had no sex in the past 12 months: 45% of men and 45% of women — and among men 20–28, 52%. Read it and weep. Not surprisingly, Japan’s fertility rate is ~1.2, South Korea 0.7, China 1.0, and India shockingly is 20 years ahead of schedule at 1.9. The two biggest countries in the world are not replacing — population and workforces will crash. The cohort of 20-year-olds in Japan is 50% of its 1948 peak.
