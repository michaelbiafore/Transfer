The AI Mania

[~0:39]

[Aside] The episode opens with a promo for Meb Faber’s series and book, Investing in America: The Rise of a 250-Year Bull Market, and a Cambria Investment Management compliance disclaimer.

Meb Faber: My guest today is Jim Grant, founder and editor of Grant’s Interest Rate Observer, which he’s been publishing since 1983 — financial historian, one of the most respected observers on Wall Street. Jim, welcome.

Jim Grant: Thank you, Meb. Nice to be here.

Meb Faber: We heard a stat that if you combine SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic potential IPOs, inflation-adjusted, they are as big as all the IPOs of the ’90s combined. The 1990s is my favorite bubble — I didn’t think we’d see anything like it again in my lifetime, but here we are. What do you think?

Jim Grant: I think the excitement surrounding the potentialities of artificial intelligence dwarfs the excitement generated by the worldwide web, the internet, and fiber-optic cables — and the dollars, even adjusted for inflation, are larger today. The role of the Fed is more intrusive, more problematical than it was then. A great deal is riding on the efficacy of the technology on which the world’s hopes are hanging, and a great deal is contingent on whether we have correctly calculated or miscalculated the demand for tokens, for data centers, for all the capital that goes into AI.

You’d think any technology with “intelligence” in the word would be up-to-date on supply and demand, but I doubt it. There’s a great deal of overbuilding and double-ordering, just like the late 1990s. People thought such was the high degree of organization of information that there’d be nothing like the macro miscalculations of yesteryear.

“But it turns out the human speculative spirit is a pretty wild thing, and is not necessarily grounded by better technology. On the contrary, sometimes that can only incite it. I think today is one of the greatest bubbles of all time.”

Railroads, 1873, and Creative Destruction

[~4:18]

Meb Faber: As a historian, do you feel special getting to experience it? What’s the better comparison — railroads, fiber optics?

Jim Grant: Railroads. We’re talking about a technology that was instrumental in setting the American price level into a generational cascade to the downside. There was a great panic in 1873 with all the usual preceding conditions of excess. The panic ended, as all panics do — but what did not end was the persistent decline in prices of all kinds, going down 2–3% a year for 20 years. People felt it keenly; there was a great hurrah in our politics about the “crime” of removing silver from our monetary system.

I reviewed a book for the Wall Street Journal — titled simply 1873, by Liaquat Ahamed (who wrote Lords of Finance). My criticism was that the author didn’t take the proper measure of the role of technology in cutting the ground out from under prices. What set prices tumbling and real wages rising was human progress — the positive destruction of time and distance with the opening of the Suez Canal in 1869. It destroyed fixed capital — the London warehouses built for the long-haul trade around the Cape of Good Hope to India — as sail gave way to steam and world commodity markets unified through rail, steam, and the telegraph. A great display of the power of creative destruction.

So here we are, many generations later, confronted with a technology about which we know very little. It can crack ancient mathematical formulas — and also fail to count milk cartons. The CFO of Uber was quoted saying, “It’s a wondrous technology, but we don’t see it in our P&L; we can’t really rationalize the expense just now.”

“What we do know is that the capital draw on AI is as great as it was on the railroads. The value proposition of railroads was simple — iron horse, what do you want? Now it is AI-augmented human intelligence planning it. All this is terrifically important and also unknowable at the moment.”

CAPE Ratios and the Long Road Back

[~8:51]

Meb Faber: Professor Shiller is famous for his 10-year P/E, but fewer people have read his papers on CAPE ratios for industrials, utilities, and railroads. In the 1920s, utilities hit a CAPE near 60 — and after the Depression, railroads got to a CAPE of two-and-change. An 80% decline will do that. Some sectors that become very dear can end up on the opposite extreme not too far away.

Jim Grant: The rails are famous for it. The stock market made its 1929 high; the industrials returned to the 1929 level only in 1954 — and that, incidentally, was the year John Kenneth Galbraith’s The Great Crash came out, a cautionary book published in the very year the Dow recaptured its 25-year highs, because the world was worried about a recurrence of 1929. People looking backward weren’t factoring in the extraordinarily cheap level of the market, or America’s place leading the recovery from the Second World War — it was set up for a demographic, industrial, technological grand slam. The industrials made their new high in 1954, but the rails (then the transports) didn’t make it until 1965 or ’66.

These transformative technologies don’t have to be so all-consuming of our attention and capital as AI or the railroads. Air conditioning was as transformative in its way — it changed migration patterns, made the Sun Belt habitable, gave us the year-round Congress, isn’t that great — yet in the ’50s, the air-conditioning stocks like Carrier traded at four, five, six, eight times earnings.

“This time of ours is in some ways very familiar — patterns conform to the usual cyclical touch points — but this one is much bigger, much more lurid than almost any preceding cycle.”

Why Deflation Is Not the Enemy

[~12:27]

Meb Faber: You’ve said: what the Fed calls deflation, a normal person would call progress. Why is everyone so scared of deflation?

Jim Grant: Deflation is a scare word. Bernanke, Ph.D., invoked it frequently during the 2007–13 period because he wanted to avoid a recurrence of the 1930s — you’ll recall him going almost on bended knee to Milton Friedman, saying, “Yeah, we did it [the Fed], and we’re not going to do it again.” But that wasn’t the foremost memory in the 1940s and ’50s. In 1954, William McChesney Martin, the Fed chairman, gave a talk on inflation and said, “You can never regain the purchasing power you have lost to inflation” — in a period when the CPI was actually showing negative year-over-year values. Nobody said, “Mr. Chairman, we’re having deflation right now.” It didn’t come up.

The key distinction: a financial deflation is the collapse of the price level owing to a collapse in credit — and by definition it entails major bank failures and credit destruction. There was nothing like that in the railroad-era deflation in this country. In 2008, the banking system was genuinely in trouble — if the government hadn’t salvaged the chestnuts from the fire, every financial institution of consequence in New York would have been toast. “The greatest failure of ratings and risk management ever.” So the inflation fear wasn’t entirely a red herring — but Bernanke didn’t do us a great turn by single-mindedly invoking the 30s to address 2009–12, pushing the funds rate down and unleashing all manner of distortions.

The Ratchet: Prices Only Go Up

[~16:34]

Jim Grant: What people fail to grasp is that, after a big inflation, it’s less about the rate of change and more that prices went up and stayed there. Your coffee went to $10 — it doesn’t matter if inflation is now 1 or 2%; coffee is still $10. It never came down.

“That’s the foremost feature of our monetary world today: only ratcheting up, never ratcheting down.”

The price level was broadly unchanged over 125 years into the 20th century — but with plenty of ups and downs. The deflation-worriers do have one argument: in a very leveraged world, falling prices can do terrific damage to balance sheets built only for prosperity. Our world is full of private credit, private equity, leveraged sovereigns, people who’ve taken out personal LBOs to buy a house. In that setting, a falling price level would be very hard to manage.

Leverage and the Trap of Where Rates Were

[~17:37]

Jim Grant: That’s the trouble with this inflationary boom. If the Fed has to raise rates to beat back rising prices, what about all the businesses capitalized for very low borrowing costs?

“Where rates were is almost as important nowadays as where rates are going.”

Rates were effectively zero in 2020–22, low enough that private equity did transactions with six or eight turns of leverage — and borrowing costs that will never again be as low as when they were incurred. People are gagging now on a funds rate below 4% and dreading 5 or 6%, which would be entirely reasonable if inflation goes to 4–4.5%. If El Niño turns out to be a problem, if the Strait of Hormuz doesn’t obey instructions from Washington — what happens then?

Private Credit and the Insurance Time Bomb

[~19:53]

Meb Faber: Corporate and junk spreads are razor-thin relative to T-bills. How much is just price-insensitive buyers — pensions, insurers — reaching for yield?

Jim Grant: That’s an element. But with insurance there’s also the Apollo–Athene model of life-insurance investing: a management style determined to minimize the equity portion (the surplus) and maximize spread income by investing heavily in securities that are speculative-grade or near it — floating-rate, in the case of private credit. Investors were reaching frantically for yield in 2020 and on, and there’s a structural change within the life-insurance industry. As the consultancy notes, a good part of the composite insurance portfolio is committed to private credit — and one-third of $6 trillion of life-insurance assets is in some form of private credit.

Meb Faber: A third of six trillion is still two trillion. It’s a lot of money when you say it fast. What’s funny is that very smart people — captains of industry, CIOs — preface their comments with, “I don’t really know what they’re doing in private credit.” You don’t hear that anywhere else.

Jim Grant: It’s not unprecedented. Remember 2005–06? “I don’t know what a CDO-squared is, but we sure have a lot of them.” There was a terrific deficit of knowledge about mortgage-credit structures before the crisis. There’s a certain opaqueness about private credit — and a certain openness from the promoters, who say, “We are doing nothing more than making good loans banks are no longer allowed to make.” I heard, only a couple of years ago, the prediction that private credit would replace banks — which hasn’t happened. To wrap up this particular speech: I don’t think it’s any more opaque or resistant to analysis than the mortgage structures were.

Grading Powell and the 2% Target

[~25:09]

Meb Faber: Give us your grade for the exiting chair — A to F.

Jim Grant: Chairman Powell doesn’t need me to remind him of his errors. He joined the board around 2010–11, from private equity. If you read the FOMC transcripts, J. Powell was forthright and on-point in his critique of the abuse of financial leverage and its risks to the macroeconomy. Then years passed, and — as happens in all these institutions — you get absorbed into the institutional worldview. He became a great deal more complacent about leverage, and he bought into the new-age idea that the Fed owed the world a 2% rate of inflation that we’d call “price stability.”

“That is Kafkaesque. People keep saying it; it doesn’t make it any less weird. 2% a year is a tax on your cash. It’s the government taking value — and Congress alone has the power to levy a tax. What’s the Fed doing?”

He bought into it in 2020, of all times — the idea that if the Fed delivered less than 2%, it owed us a rate higher than 2% so it would average 2% over time — on the very eve of inflation going to 9% in the stimulus spike. A terribly mistimed and misconceived call. At one point he said we’d “have to unlearn what we learned about the money supply” as M2 was rising 20% year-over-year.

I don’t mean to pettifog the departing chairman — I’ve committed plenty of errors myself. His problem was insufficient detachment from the institutional view of the Fed. I do admire how he kept his dignity in the face of attacks from the man who appointed him. One real fault: he got behind the powers intent on barring Judy Shelton from the board — she wanted the Fed to have some grounding in the discipline of gold. I believe Powell actively resisted her candidacy, which was political malpractice, if you ask me — which you did.

Kevin Warsh, Gold, and Celebrity Central Bankers

[~29:47]

Jim Grant: Kevin Warsh was at our conference last year. The rule is: anyone who sits down with me for an interview goes on the federal payroll — before that, Scott Bessent was the featured guest and he was hired by the government too. I blew the Warsh interview: the first thing I asked was whether it bothered him that the Fed, if it kept its books by GAAP instead of its DIY system, is “solvent only by courtesy” — actually broke. Kevin said, “Well, Jim, it’s a pleasure to be here, ladies and gentlemen.” So I lost the moral edge. But as we left the stage — I’d put in a word for my hobbyhorse, gold — Kevin said, “Jim, you can’t go back.” He does want a smaller Fed balance sheet and a less obtrusive central bank.

“If the Fed were doing its job properly, we would not know the names of these people, as we know the names of the leading RBI champions in the National League. The central bankers have become celebrities — a sign of the deterioration in our monetary affairs.”

I once offered on Twitter to chair the Fed — my only input would be to peg the funds rate to the 2-year note, call it a day, drink a few beers, and reconvene next quarter, letting the market figure it out. They haven’t called.

Too Much Money: Inflation as Overstraining

[~32:19]

Meb Faber: You’ve said it’s not “too much money chasing too few goods” — it’s just too much money, and where it chases is variable: sometimes your cappuccino, sometimes New York real estate, sometimes stocks, sometimes farmland. I just met an adviser whose client sold farmland for $300,000 an acre — to an AI data center, who’s less price-sensitive than the corn farmer down the street.

Jim Grant: I did say it, and I think inflation is a matter of overstraining generally — the productive apparatus, the labor market, the financial system. A question of too-muchness, which is largely a function of monetary indiscipline. In a paper-money regime without the discipline of convertibility, there’s a permanent element of helium — things want to go up. In the 1990s, when labor was outsourced to India and China entered the WTO, goods were cheap and there was little measured inflation — but there was a lot of inflation “at the corner of Broad and Wall.”

You can measure inflation by core PCE, trimmed-mean core CPI — all manner of recondite price indices — and pick out the ones that make it look tame. McChesney Martin would identify excessive valuations and leverage in the stock market as a species of inflation. Today, because of the reign of the econometricians, there’s much less willingness to take a qualitative view. Look at the IPO market; margin debt has roughly doubled in the past year; the Chicago Fed’s Financial Conditions Index says easy money. So why would you want to cut rates? Well — because somebody says so.

The Fed’s Hidden Constraint

[~37:05]

Meb Faber: Could inflation tick up while bond rates rise too, putting the Fed in a pickle? Do they just let it happen and inflate away the debt? Rob Arnott showed that when inflation runs above 4–5%, the average CAPE is in the low teens — and we’re at 43 today.

Jim Grant: I think they’ll be very attentive to the stability of credit. Nobody gives a speech warning of a materially higher funds rate in this time of high leverage and precarious goings-on in, for example, the life-insurance business — but that’s what we’ve been writing about. The Fed is constrained in ways it does not acknowledge — constrained against an aggressive attack on inflation in a time of extreme financial leverage. That leverage now also describes the federal finances: you can easily imagine the government issuing T-bills to service the existing stock of T-bills — borrowing money to meet interest expense. That’s called Ponzi finance.

The more immediate problem is lower-rated corporate borrowers rolling over debt incurred at very low rates — especially at 100–200 basis points higher. Officially the Fed has a dual mandate; there’s a little-noted third (stable, moderate long-term rates); but the fourth is Wall Street — financial stability, the 401(k) mandate.

“Do you really want to be the guy who sank the stock market from a CAPE ratio where it is today? The Fed is up against the valuations it does not discuss, the margin debt it does not discuss, and most of all the leverage its own suppressed interest rates instigated. Much of it is its own doing.”

How to Fix the Federal Reserve

[~41:10]

Meb Faber: Any president gives you 10 minutes. What would you tell them?

Jim Grant: A few things I’ve always thought the Fed needs to do: be less obtrusive, run a smaller balance sheet, ditch the dual mandate, concentrate on really stable prices, acknowledge it is broke as an item of financial hygiene — and put some gold in its basement as a marker of its commitment to sound finance.

We also need to restore personal responsibility to financial outcomes. Part of the ethos of the gold standard was the double-liability rule: bank stockholders were themselves responsible for the bank’s solvency — they’d get a capital call, not the FDIC (of which there was none). That institution went out the window around 1935, but it spoke well of our ancestors: they realized banks are precarious by nature of their leverage, that stockholders got the dividends on the upside and ought, in justice, to front the capital on the downside.

The currency ought to be redeemable as a marker of the government’s commitment to fiscal probity — not a 2%-a-year tax, but actual stable prices in money you can hold in your hand. I’m under no illusions about reinstituting the gold standard, but the way forward is to announce a resumption of the ideals of the generation of Hamilton and Gallatin.

“Forty-five years ago, Reagan got on television and said the public debt had just hit $1 trillion. We’ve gone from $1 trillion in principal to $1 trillion of interest expense in a lifetime. We’re paying a trillion dollars in interest this fiscal year. $39 trillion gross public debt — okay, that’s normal now.”

Gold: A 40-Year Story

[~45:16]

Meb Faber: You’ve owned gold for 40 years. A lot of young folks got seduced by “digital gold,” Bitcoin. Talk about gold’s role in a portfolio.

Jim Grant: Here’s my gold origin story. I was standing in line — mistake number one; never queue up for an investment. It was a chilly day early in 1980, close to the peak of $850 an ounce. I’d persuaded myself this was the monetary moment of crisis — the CPI was up 13%, some people paid 20% for a mortgage. As I pushed my cashier’s check across for $875, I thought, “Ah, I am protected.” Fast forward a few years: with four children and tuitions to pay, I had to confess, much to my mortification, that the Krugerrand or two I’d “wasted the family cash on” had gone from $850 to about $500.

“That’s something to know about gold as an investment: it can go to sleep for 15 or 20 years, leaving you feeling very foolish. It pays nothing, earns nothing — it is the Warren Buffett idea of a bad investment; Charlie Munger heaped scorn on it. The bear case is in some ways irrefutable.”

Gold is a conceptual investment in the willful, orchestrated, managed decline of America’s dollar. Why did it go from $2,800 to $5,600 in 18 months? I don’t know — the conditions were there. Now it’s around $4,400. Somehow the world got it into its mind that war is bearish for gold. No — war is bullish for gold, with the other horsemen of the apocalypse. Gold will insist on doing what it ought not. Artfully picking the interval, it’s outperformed stocks over 20 years — except not every year, and except you often felt rather foolish owning it. I own it because I believe the dollar has gone 99% down against gold.

Peculiarly, it’s central banks — the very printers of irredeemable currency — who’ve been the big buyers, Asia much more than the West, while American individuals largely haven’t joined in. Gold yields exactly as much as Bitcoin (nothing), but its pedigree is better and it’s less volatile — though lately it’s shown some of “the speculative bounciness of the crypto world, which I find undignified for the ancient monetary metal.”

Building a Portfolio in 2026

[~52:39]

Meb Faber: 2026 feels fraught — expensive stocks, risky bonds. T-bills and gold? Other assets?

Jim Grant: I don’t set up as a financial adviser, so take this with a grain of salt — but I invest with people who find a great deal to do. At Grant’s, every two weeks we publish a security analysis, and we often find longs despite the elevated CAPE: obscure European banks trading at astonishingly low multiples of book and earnings; a UK platform for used-car transactions that the market is sure will be cancelled by AI, where we see reasons to doubt that. We’ve also taken a shine to oil, believing the supply-demand balance favors the long side of hydrocarbons whatever happens in Iran — a war that’s beginning to feel like a grudge match in professional wrestling: the violence felt keenly by those in the way, but otherwise oddly performative.

Jim’s Most Memorable Investment

[~54:36]

Jim Grant: The year is 2009, and Mr. Market is not feeling well. I recalled Sir John Templeton, who in 1939–40 — as the Battle of Britain raged and France had been overrun — bought shares of everything below two quid in London. So I called the broker and said, “Put about $5,000 into all the S&P stocks trading below $10.” (That was during the “genius phase” of Grant’s, which ended shortly after that phone call.) Some of these things were up 20-fold. It speaks to the extraordinary depths and heights Mr. Market can reach.

“The late, great Dick Russell — who wrote the Dow Theory Letters — had a humble axiom: markets can do anything. Isn’t that good to remember? Just as soon as you think you’ve got it all figured out, markets can do anything.”

Markets Can Do Anything

[~56:19]

Jim Grant: A great investor who recently died, Murray Stahl — who founded Horizon Kinetics (Steve Bregman and colleagues now carry it on) — was an unconventional thinker. Not the conceited kind of contrarian (“just because everyone’s carrying an umbrella doesn’t mean it’s not going to rain”), but a real thinker. He believed the virtues of diversification were overdone, because diversification means you don’t know enough about what you own. One Horizon Kinetics fund had a single 60%-of-AUM position — and it worked out.

My first book was a biography of Bernard Baruch, the great speculator, who said:

“I have lost money enough to make the average married man go out and shoot himself.”

Baruch taught me the importance of sucking it up, bearing your losses, not overly blaming yourself — but resolving to make a different mistake next time. What makes all of this so interesting is the utter unpredictability of the human animal in the face of large sums of money. We’re not at our most rational. We “know” AI will conquer all; we “know” Elon Musk can’t possibly die broke — yet there are trillions of leveraged dollars running on factually indefensible convictions that may be true, may not be true, but are certainly not certainly true.

The Lesson of 1984

[~1:00:01]

Meb Faber: One period in history these youngsters should read about?

Jim Grant: 1984 — a laboratory experiment in the bond market and the world’s perception of it. Interest rates had peaked at 15% in a 35-year bond bear market (1946–81). Paul Volcker lowered the boom; Reagan fired the air-traffic controllers, setting a new era in labor relations; commodity prices — oil especially — tanked. The inflation cycle was, obviously, over. But maybe not so obviously: money supply picked up, monetarism ruled the roost, and in the spring of 1984 the 30-year Treasury briefly traded at 14% while the CPI was printing about 4% —

“Giving that mythical, clever speculator who bought at exactly 14% ten percentage points of real yield. Holy mackerel. That’s a one-decision bond — you just go away the rest of your lifetime. And Wall Street made it easy, selling zero-coupon instruments called CATS, LIONS, and TIGRs with internal rates of return of 12–14%.”

Why did people pass it up? Because they recollected the long, brutal preceding bear market. I was writing at the time, trying to think of reasons to be bullish on bonds, and the bears would say, “I’ve seen bonds go down 10 points a year — what are you talking about?” That episode is the clearest example of the perversity of markets — what is so compellingly a bargain in retrospect was, at the time, anything but. By 1986 everyone had to have bonds; imagine telling them they’d one day yield near zero, some sovereigns negative — you’d have been read out of the Wall Street community of knowing people.

Jim Grant: What a pleasure. Thank you, Meb.

Key Takeaways

Concept Summary
One of the greatest bubbles of all time AI’s excitement dwarfs the dot-com era, with bigger inflation-adjusted dollars and a more intrusive Fed. The human speculative spirit isn’t grounded by better technology — sometimes it’s incited by it.
The railroad / 1873 analogy A transformative technology (rail, steam, the Suez Canal) drove prices down 2–3% a year for 20 years — creative destruction. AI’s capital draw rivals the railroads, but its payoff is unknowable.
Sectors fall as far as they rise After 1929, industrials took until 1954 to recover and rails until 1965–66. Air conditioning was transformative yet its stocks traded at 4–8× earnings.
Deflation ≠ disaster What the Fed calls deflation, a normal person calls progress. The dangerous kind is financial deflation — a credit collapse with bank failures (2008), not the benign price decline of the railroad era.
The ratchet Inflation’s lasting harm isn’t the rate of change — it’s that prices went up and stayed up. “$10 coffee never came back.” Only ratcheting up, never down.
Where rates were matters Near-zero rates in 2020–22 loaded the system with leverage at costs that will never recur. That leverage is now the economy’s binding constraint.
Private credit echoes 2008 One-third of $6 trillion in life-insurance assets sits in private credit (Apollo–Athene model). “I don’t know what they’re doing” rhymes with the 2005–06 CDO-squared era.
Powell’s flaw Insufficient detachment from the institutional Fed view — and buying the “Kafkaesque” idea that 2% inflation is price stability. “2% a year is a tax on your cash.”
Celebrity central bankers “If the Fed were doing its job, we wouldn’t know these people’s names.” Grant’s fix: peg the funds rate to the 2-year note and reconvene next quarter.
Inflation = too much money Not “too much money chasing too few goods” but overstraining — and where the money lands is variable (cappuccinos, NYC real estate, stocks, $300k/acre farmland).
The Fed’s hidden constraint It cannot aggressively fight inflation amid extreme leverage. Federal finances verge on “Ponzi” — borrowing to pay interest; $1tn principal in 1980 → $1tn of interest today.
How to fix the Fed Smaller balance sheet, single mandate (stable prices), admit insolvency, hold gold, and restore personal responsibility (the gold-era double-liability rule).
Gold A conceptual bet on the managed decline of the dollar — pays nothing, can “go to sleep for 15–20 years.” War is bullish, not bearish. Central banks (esp. Asia) are the big buyers.
Where Grant finds value (2026) Obscure European banks, a UK used-car platform the market thinks AI will kill, and long oil — despite a CAPE of 43.
Markets can do anything 2009: sub-$10 S&P stocks went up 20-fold. Diversification can mean not knowing what you own (Murray Stahl’s 60% position). “Resolve to make a different mistake next time.” (Baruch)
The lesson of 1984 A 14% 30-year Treasury at ~4% CPI offered 10 points of real yield — a “one-decision bond” — yet was shunned because of the prior bear market. The perversity of markets in one chart.
