The Meeting

1. My IAS Visit

[~0:00]

This is a special, inaugural solo episode of the Cool Worlds podcast, and I felt compelled to share it because a recent meeting is transforming the way I think about the future of science. It was one of the most impactful scientific meetings I’ve ever been to.

I happened to be visiting Princeton on the Tuesday to give the astronomy colloquium. The following day they packed me off two miles down the road to the Institute for Advanced Study (IAS). There was a coffee meeting at 11 a.m. led by one of the senior astrophysics faculty — somebody of enormous scientific stature, whose identity I’ll protect. My attendance was accidental, fortuitous; it was an internal meeting, not an open call.

It was fairly shocking to attend. A lot of what I’m about to tell you I had somewhat internalized already — but what was so shocking was that all of this was said out loud. Everybody was saying the same thing. We were all in the same chorus.

2. IAS Context

[~0:37]

These are people I hold in the highest esteem, coming to similar conclusions as me.

3. Why This Setting Matters

[~2:25]

The IAS setting matters to this story. It’s one of the most elite intellectual institutes on planet Earth. This is where Einstein worked; where Oppenheimer worked. Walking down the corridor I passed Ed Witten — a father of string theory and one of the most brilliant living minds. That context gives credence to what I’m about to tell you.

These are big voices. This is not Silicon Valley tech bros, not billionaires, not hype. These are people who built their careers on analytic thinking, mathematical development, and abstract thought. True thought leaders.

4. Meeting Brief

[~3:43]

The meeting was led primarily by one senior faculty member, who spent maybe 30–40 minutes discussing agentic AI — Claude, Cursor, a little ChatGPT — and how he uses it in his research and life (organizing travel, emails), but primarily research. He showed many examples of research projects achieved with just a few prompts, delivering very impressive results at the cutting edge of what we’d expect for a scientific paper. A historian was present on Zoom and commented toward the end, after which there was an open forum of discussion in which I participated.

The Concessions

5. Coding Supremacy

[~5:00]

The first big shocker — though to me it wasn’t a shocker so much as jarring to hear said aloud — was that AI models have now achieved complete coding supremacy over humans in software development.

Remember the audience: astrophysicists. Not software developers by title, but people who do enormous amounts of it — large cosmological simulations like Enzo, Illustris, Gadget, hydrodynamic codes with adaptive mesh grids. These are very talented coders.

There was not a single hand that objected. Widespread concession that AI was not just better than humans — it had complete supremacy. The phrase “order of magnitude superior” was used in the room.

It hit me that even people far better at coding than I am have just given up and conceded the point.

6. Analytic Supremacy

[~6:01]

It wasn’t isolated to coding. There was also a concession that analytic reasoning, problem-solving, and mathematics were at least comparable to — and perhaps superior to — the abilities of the people in that room (and I challenge you to find a room with a higher average IQ).

The earlier models (3.5, 4) often hallucinated mathematical solutions, and that still happens to some degree, but it’s becoming less and less frequent and the results more trustworthy. As a personal anecdote: in a recent set of derivations, Mathematica could not solve an integral, but ChatGPT (5.2) could — and not only did it solve it (I checked it numerically), it provided the entire derivation, every substitution and rearrangement, which Mathematica does not.

The lead faculty said these models can already, in a very broad intellectual sense, do something like 90% of the things he can do. He wasn’t sure of the number — it could be 60%, it could be 99% — but it was clearly a majority, and only going to grow.

7. Surrendering Control

[~10:14]

This may be unique to the senior faculty leader, though a few hands agreed: he had totally surrendered control of his digital life to agentic AI — handing over emails, file system, calendars, full superuser access to his personal machines and servers. About a third of the room said they’d installed similar agentic systems.

I haven’t done that — I’ve never even used Claude or Cursor, just a web portal to ChatGPT — which made me feel behind the curve. When people asked him, aren’t you uncomfortable handing your entire life to these machines, have you read the privacy terms?

He just said: “I don’t care.” The advantage it affords me is so outsized that it’s irrelevant that I’m losing these privacy controls. And that’s not an extreme view — people in the room agreed.

The Embrace

8. Accelerating Adoption

[~12:33]

This wasn’t an institutional endorsement — just an informal discussion — but they were embracing it, asking how can we accelerate adoption. One purpose of the meeting was to form a group where everyone could learn to install these systems quickly, set up skills and rules, and do prompt engineering.

The sentiment: we’re not trying to resist this. Get on the train. This is not something to resist — embrace it.

9. Ethics Be Damned

[~13:43]

An obvious counter is ethics — billionaires, job replacement, the power and water consumption, climate effects. The senior faculty acknowledged all of it, and then (paraphrasing): those concerns exist, but I don’t care, because the advantage is too great.

It was, essentially, ethics be damned. And I don’t think it was an isolated view — there was a sense that you have to do this to be competitive, and the advantages so overwhelmed the concerns.

10. Skill Atrophy

[~15:08]

A concern raised more in the Q&A was skill atrophy. My analogy: GPS. Fifteen or twenty years ago we had a better sense of direction — a 3D map in our minds. With navigation machines, we defer and stop thinking about where we’re going. Software development is already gone that way; mathematical and analytic skills are next; and more general still, problem-solving itself, because with agentic AI you just guide it, define the problem, and it does the solving.

This is a bit like Adam and Eve and the forbidden fruit. The serpent is the AI companies — here, grab this apple. Once you do, you’ve lost innocence. There’s no way back, because of skill atrophy. We have little choice but to do it if we want to be competitive — yet we’re cognizant of the deep concerns about our own intellectual abilities.

11. Use AI Else You’re Cooked

[~17:20]

The case for adoption rested on a sense of inevitability. If the top institutions use these systems and you don’t, you simply won’t be competitive in the avalanche of discovery that may be coming. Adoption is, in that sense, unavoidable.

12. Learning to Use AI

[~18:48]

This is about human training, not the models’ training sets. The first time you use one of these models it’s often frustrating — “crap comes out the other end” — and because it talks like a human, you get emotionally angry at it and chuck it out the window. The lead faculty admitted he’d spent a long time trial-and-erroring how to use these systems correctly. It’s about developing AI fluency: how to set up a problem, structure a workflow, build skills.

Early adopters who invest the time now will have a significant edge. That’s exactly why the group wanted to set up an accelerator to train interested scientists.

13. Human Oversight

[~20:43]

This was peppered throughout the whole discussion: the results aren’t always reliable — they still hallucinate, though it’s improving — so humans must cross-check. Looking at plots, running snippets of code, interpreting and discussing results. The brain is still activated, but it’s a very different intellectual activity — more like being a mentor, advisor, or manager than working the problem yourself.

Notably, he was doing many cross-checks between AI models — running a problem through Cursor, then Claude, then discussing the results in ChatGPT, making sure they agreed. On transparency: with Cursor you can see the diffs; with Claude he described sub-agents solving parts of the problem under the hood, which is less transparent. He first favored Cursor for that visibility — but as his trust grew, he found the transparency an annoyance, and switched to Claude because it’s faster and can go further with fewer prompts.

If there are ways to dial human oversight back to a minimum, that’s already happening. Extrapolate that trend — who knows where the human-oversight role even persists?

The Economics

14. Cost Concerns

[~23:42]

I raised cost in the meeting. ChatGPT Plus is ~$20/month, but Claude and Cursor add up — the senior faculty was already spending hundreds of dollars a month of his own money. That already prices out early-career people and those at less-resourced institutions, introducing inequity.

The deeper worry is exploding cost. Like every subscription service (Netflix, software), prices creep up. Imagine a world where these models are cheap now, we all get addicted, our skills atrophy — and then two or three years in they pull the rug and charge several thousand a month. Many will pay, because the field’s expectation (the Overton window) will have shifted about what “normal” productivity is.

On the scale of the bet: AI investment since ~2014 in the top three or four companies is now over five times (inflation-adjusted) that of the entire Apollo program, and over fifty times the Manhattan Project. I don’t think there’s ever been a human endeavor we’ve invested so much into. That money has to come back.

15. Patents & IP

[~28:01]

(This came up over lunch — and by the way, once that coffee meeting happened, the entire day — lunches, coffees, dinner — was dominated by AI.) One way these companies could recover their investment, and why they’re pivoting toward science (Sam Altman on fusion power, drug discovery), is to claim IP and patent shares. Imagine a “GPT Pro / research-grade” tier where the terms of service mean you give up 10%, 20%, half of your IP to the company.

But if they have to reclaim a ~$2 trillion hole, how can the promised “age of abundance” — cheap fusion, free drugs — actually be cheap? Surely they’ll have to charge a lot. And if so, you’d have to question the whole point of doing this in the first place.

16. Who Wins?

[~32:32]

Traditionally, those with an edge in a technical field like astrophysics have superior technical skills — solving differential equations, abstract math, coding advanced simulations. A lot of those advantages are now neutralized by these models. So the people who succeed going forward will be a different breed than the last few centuries of science have produced.

The New York Flatiron Institute has a Center for Computational Astrophysics (CCA) that deliberately propped up software developers — a skill long underappreciated in the academic job market. But with coding supremacy, what happens to those jobs? Maybe they just look different — people who manage software development done by agentic AIs.

Wealth also matters: if models eventually charge per unit of reasoning, a massive endowment (Harvard, Princeton, Columbia) becomes an advantage — an almost oligarchic disruption of research institutions. The people who thrive will be those who can compartmentalize problems, have patience (these models are frustrating — the lead faculty admitted spending hours “all caps screaming” at them), and bring managerial skills. That may be what the next generation of super-scientists looks like.

Who Gets Left Behind

17. Who Loses?

[~34:05]

People with inherent technical brilliance aren’t advantaged anymore. And we’re already seeing losers in industry — junior positions and internships evaporating because it’s far cheaper to use a model than hire someone. That could proliferate into academic training: fewer graduate students, PhDs, even undergraduates. Three reasons it might happen:

  1. Time. Training a scientist is an enormous investment. At the end of a five-year PhD you have a fully formed peer; on day one their face goes blank — they know almost nothing. You re-run that cycle for every student, hours of meetings each week. Models don’t need that — they work out of the tin. A first-year project we’d give a student a year to solve, a model does in a few prompts.
  2. Cost. A graduate student costs my grants ~$100,000/year (with tuition and health insurance) — typical at top institutions. Compare that to ~$20/month. That’s an ocean apart, and in today’s environment of severe grant cuts, the incentives compound.
  3. Morality vs. futility. You could argue we have an ethical responsibility to train scientists — that the scientist who goes into society is the real product, and I believe that. But the counterweight (which I’m not advocating): why spend five years training a human scientist “when those beings won’t even exist in five years”?

I’m not advocating any of this. But early-career scientists are at greater risk of being displaced, and we care deeply about keeping the flame of research going to the next generation.

18. Grad Admissions

[~40:30]

At Columbia we get ~400 applications for four or five PhD positions — roughly a 1% admission rate. Narrowing that to a long list of 30–40 is a huge, contentious time sink. Two AI angles:

  • The senior faculty conceded they use AI to assist grad admissions (to what degree was unclear), and he felt it was the fastest, most accurate, most reliable he’d ever felt about admissions. Meanwhile applicants are surely using ChatGPT too — the English/language boost for international students is obvious. So it’s already happening on both sides.
  • What skills are we even selecting for? There’s no agreement — collaborative ability, technical/simulation skill, communication, research experience. We’ve used largely traditional criteria for ~100 years, and you have to wonder whether the people we select have the right skill set to thrive five years from now.

I’ve started wondering: would I agree to work with a student who refused to use these models? As hard as it is to confess — it’s like working with someone who refuses to use the internet or any programming language. Two hands tied behind your back. That’s not quite true yet, but it’s on the cusp.

19. Collaborations

[~45:47]

Scientists need things from each other — everyone has a finite skill set. But collaboration has friction: you reach out, email, chat at a conference, set up multi-hour Zoom sessions. Especially for something small — “I just need this one tiny calculation” — that’s a lot of time for both parties, and you can see AI being preferable.

So I wonder whether collaborations will shrink to just the core people you really need — and eventually maybe even single-author papers (I write those often; it might become more common). I’ll acknowledge some people do science for the collaboration — but for the many introverts in science, and for the small asks especially, this could diminish collaboration.

20. Tenured Faculty

[~48:43]

Everyone in the room acknowledged that tenured faculty will be fine — if there are victims, tenured faculty are the last people on the boat, going down with the ship (the institutions themselves) by the very definition of tenure.

But I don’t pretend my job is infinitely safe — the whole point of this video is that it could go, and might be one of the first to go. With the caveat that I’m not an economist: I’m skeptical AI displaces all jobs (physical labor, no), but intellectual labor is unusually vulnerable, and scientists are doing exactly that. The hard, unresolved balance for mentors: you don’t want brain-dead students who just type into a prompt and never think — but you do want thoughtful use that boosts productivity. I do not know how to find that balance, but it’s important that we try.

The Personal Reckoning

21. Me & AI

[~51:58]

Let me be totally transparent about my own AI use, because some of you get upset when I use an AI thumbnail or visual — “AI slop.” I’ve been using AI for over a decade — in fact developing AI models (deep neural networks to predict the stability of circumbinary planets and find missing planets in multi-planet systems). I stopped developing models ~8–9 years ago, not for ethical reasons but because I couldn’t keep pace with the literature while staying broad.

But I still use the tools constantly: proof-checking papers (paste in the LaTeX source for factual/grammatical errors), vibe coding (though I probably still write most of my own code), debugging (I almost never debug myself anymore — paste the error, “what’s wrong with this?”), proposal and report revisions, literature search (it does hallucinate references, but those are easy to Google-check), and derivations. For the TARS project I leaned on it heavily for the material science I didn’t know — tensile strengths, graphene properties, albedo, atom-layer thicknesses.

I’m not a super-user like the faculty leading that discussion. My scientific strength has always been creativity, and I think that’s actually supercharged by these models.

On the video side, to be honest: we use DX Revive to clean audio, lalal.ai to strip background music from B-roll, Rev.com for transcripts, Topaz for upscaling, and ChatGPT for fact-checking scripts and brainstorming titles. We’ve used a gen-AI model for an entire thumbnail visual maybe two or three times, but more commonly it’s removing/padding a background. My narrative videos (Journey to the End of the Universe, The First Civilization) used a commissioned artist’s five images, which I had to stretch and re-crop — and these models would let my editor Joe and me make narratives we thought impossible without a full production crew. I’m hesitant, though, given the strong reaction against AI visuals.

22. Public Backlash

[~58:40]

There’s a strong public reaction — the common comments being “AI slop” and “AI is evil.” I hate AI slop too — to me that’s a whole video that’s just spam, reading out Wikipedia, recycling the same old junk. I only make a video if I feel I have something totally unique to say, which is why I don’t do news videos. So I’d defend that our content isn’t slop — but I genuinely want to hear if you feel we shouldn’t use it at all.

On “AI is evil”: you can make that argument, and I’m sympathetic — like social media’s negative effects (though I’ve always weirdly exempted YouTube, given its educational value). But technological change has always displaced jobs — if you groomed horses when Henry Ford invented the car, you’d rightly worry, but society shouldn’t pivot to protect horse-groomers. That’s not progress. Some routine video-production tasks may get displaced, but that doesn’t obviously make it evil.

One thing I wrote down: the people in that IAS room were largely oblivious to public backlash — far more than someone like me, with a foot in both worlds. They weren’t worried that a paper 99% generated by AI would bother the public, because they didn’t see AI-assisted science as illegitimate — its skills are already comparable, if not superior, to their own.

23. Historical Significance

[~63:42]

The historian in the room explicitly framed this as a transitional period for society — a historic moment that future historians will look back on. There was a giggle when he said it, but he urged: we should be documenting what’s happening right now.

That’s exactly what inspired this video — to document what this moment feels like, because it’s so different from a few years ago. I’m sure in five years I’ll look back and see how much I got wrong. But a lot of what I’ve told you isn’t just in my head — it’s a common sentiment held by the most elite brains in astrophysics.

24. Democratization of Science

[~65:21]

Science is normally done by people like me — the product of relentless filtering, the rarefied air at the top of the ivory tower. That might change. Many of you email me brilliant ideas I never have time to pursue. Soon you won’t need me: you can consult these models for $20 a month and write a research paper yourself, and it could easily be comparable in quality to what someone with two decades of training produces. That’s a genuine democratization of science — anyone can participate at a scale never before possible.

The downside is the paper tsunami. arXiv already posts dozens of new astronomy papers a day — you can’t read them, you scan titles and abstracts. With democratization plus super-users doing 3–4× more papers per year, we could face one or two orders of magnitude more papers. How do we ingest that? “Use AI to read them” isn’t the same thing — I need it in my brain to be a scientist (short of a Neuralink).

25. Science as a Human Endeavour

[~69:09]

Why do we even do science? For me, the reason I love it is that I’m a participating actor — I like finding things out. It’s like being a detective at a crime scene; you don’t want the spoiler, you want to play along and figure it out yourself.

I don’t know what enjoyment I’d have in a world where humans are cut out of the discovery process. Science is a bit like art: AI-generated art has its place, but the art I’m most compelled by is the human story — what motivated the artist, what was going on in their life. Science is intrinsically a human endeavour, done for sheer curiosity and love.

If super-intelligence delivers fusion and new physics but the discoveries are incomprehensible to any human — no one understands how the fusion machine works — there’s economic benefit, but it subtracts from a multi-millennial human drive to understand the world. I don’t want to live in a world where everything around me is just magic, a fantasy world. I want a comprehensible world.

Final Thoughts

[~72:13]

This is very unlike our usual videos — live, unscripted, raw — but I think it’s a conversation we have to have, and one I haven’t seen done quite this way before.

The real kicker: this isn’t just in my head. The most elite institutes in the world are having emergency internal meetings about this, and the smartest people you can name are worried — they see it as a threat to their intellectual supremacy and have already conceded much of that ground. This is really happening. We’re not waiting for it down the pipe — we’re swimming in it.

Please let me know your thoughts — this is a conversation not just with scientists but with the public, because you fund what we do, and the point of science is public benefit. It’s relevant to the future of this channel, to science itself, and to society. Thank you for your patience to the end. And as always — stay thoughtful and stay curious.
